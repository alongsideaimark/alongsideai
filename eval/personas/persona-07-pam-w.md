### Section 2 — Your Work

**What do you do for work?**

I'm the managing partner of a small family law practice. Divorce, custody, equitable distribution, occasionally adoptions. We handle South Carolina cases primarily, almost all in the Lowcountry — Charleston, Berkeley, and Dorchester counties.

**Do you run your own business? Tell us about it.**

I do. I founded the firm in 2008. Today we are four attorneys including me, two paralegals, and one administrative assistant. We did about $2.1M in revenue last year. The practice is healthy by any reasonable measure — we have more demand than we can take on, and we've had to start turning away consultations. The challenge is internal: our office manager of nine years retired in November, and I have not replaced her, and the cracks are showing.

**What does a typical workday look like for you?**

Mornings are court days when I have hearings — temporary hearings, motions, the occasional trial day. Otherwise, mornings are reserved for client meetings and consultations. Afternoons I'm reviewing my associates' work, drafting on my own files, and dealing with whatever administrative crisis has presented itself. I leave the office at 6:30 most days and do another hour of email at home after dinner.

### Section 3 — What's Frustrating

**What takes way longer than it should in your day?**

New client intake. We get inquiries from our website, from referrals, and from a couple of paid sources. Right now intake is essentially: someone calls or emails, the admin takes a message, I or one of my associates calls them back, we book a consultation, we run a conflict check, we do the consultation, we send an engagement letter and a trust account request, we wait for the retainer to clear. The end-to-end takes anywhere from three days to two weeks per new client and we lose people in the gaps.

**Is there anything you avoid doing because it's confusing or tedious?**

Reviewing our marketing analytics. I have a marketing consultant who manages our Google profile and a small ad budget. He sends a monthly report. I look at the cost per lead number and that's about it. I know I should understand more.

**What do you wish someone would just handle for you?**

Calendar management. Since the office manager left, my calendar is being maintained by my paralegal as a side responsibility, and we are double-booked at least once a week. It would be a relief to have a single competent system that the whole firm uses.

**Have you ever thought "there has to be a better way to do this"? What was it about?**

Document drafting. Family law has a tremendous amount of repeat boilerplate — financial declarations, parenting plans, separation agreements. We use Clio's document automation features but only halfway. Junior attorneys still rebuild documents from a prior matter rather than from a template, which means our documents drift in quality.

### Section 5 — What You've Tried

**Have you tried any AI tools before?** Yes — ChatGPT (free)

**If yes, what was your experience?**

I have used it for things like rewording a difficult email to opposing counsel and for summarizing a long deposition I didn't have time to re-read. I would not use it to draft anything that touches a client's legal position without significant verification.

**Is there anything about AI that makes you nervous or skeptical?**

Confidentiality is the obvious one. Anything that touches client information has to be handled carefully — I have professional responsibility obligations, and I am not interested in being on the receiving end of a Bar complaint because I uploaded a financial declaration into the wrong system. I am also concerned about hallucinated citations. There is now case law on attorneys getting sanctioned for filing AI-generated briefs with fake cases. We are not going there.
