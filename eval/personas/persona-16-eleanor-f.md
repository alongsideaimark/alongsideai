### Section 2 — Your Work

**What do you do for work?**

I retired in 2022 from a 35-year career in arts marketing. I was VP of marketing at a major orchestra for the last 14 years of that. Now I write a Substack called Old Records — essays about classical music, mostly historical recordings, with some new releases when something catches my ear.

**Do you run your own business? Tell us about it.**

The Substack is the closest thing. About 1,800 free subscribers, around 80 paid at $7 a month. So roughly $560 a month in revenue, which is plant-watering money, not a business. But it's growing — I started in late 2023 and have about doubled in the last year.

**What does a typical workday look like for you?**

I write about three mornings a week. I publish twice a week — Tuesday and Friday. The rest of my mornings are concert listening (often with notes), reading, and the occasional research trip into Manhattan to a library or an archive. Afternoons are walks, my husband (a retired law professor), and reading. I don't work in any traditional sense and yet I work most days.

### Section 3 — What's Frustrating

**What takes way longer than it should in your day?**

Promoting the Substack. I post on the Substack platform itself, and on Notes, and occasionally on Twitter (still cannot bring myself to call it X), and I've had a Bluesky account for six months I haven't used. The cross-posting is messy and I'm not getting compounding effects from any of it.

**Is there anything you avoid doing because it's confusing or tedious?**

The data side of the Substack. Substack provides analytics that I look at and don't fully understand, and I'm certain there's signal in there about what to write more of. I look at the numbers and feel vaguely scolded.

**What do you wish someone would just handle for you?**

Engaging with comments. The Substack has built a small but real community in the comments and I am not as responsive as I should be. I'd love a way to keep up that didn't feel like a part-time job.

**Have you ever thought "there has to be a better way to do this"? What was it about?**

Researching pieces. A typical essay involves listening to four or five recordings, reading three or four old reviews, often digging through specific archive sites that have terrible search functions. I've thought about building a personal database. I've also thought about hiring a research assistant. I've done neither.

### Section 5 — What You've Tried

**Have you tried any AI tools before?** Yes — ChatGPT and Claude, occasionally

**If yes, what was your experience?**

Mixed. ChatGPT gave me some surprisingly bad information about a specific 1962 recording I asked about — confidently wrong. Claude has been better for genuine questions about music history. Both are useful for editing — I use them as a second reader before I publish.

**Is there anything about AI that makes you nervous or skeptical?**

The voice question. My readers come for my voice. If I let an AI smooth my prose, I lose the readers. I'm careful to use these tools for editing and never for drafting.
