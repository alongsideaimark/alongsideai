01 — A note to start

Thank you for trusting us with the honest version.

You shared where things get stuck. We read everything carefully. What follows is a plan written for you — not a template — and yours to keep whether you decide to continue or not.

We don't sell software, and we don't make anything on the side from any of the tools we recommend. That means we only suggest things we'd use ourselves, and only when the math clearly works for you. If a recommendation below doesn't feel right, say so — we'll happily rework it.

Everything here is a suggestion, not a commitment. You can do all of it, some of it, or none of it.

02 — Your world today

Here's what we understood from your answers.

Not problems — just observations. If we got anything wrong, tell us and we'll fix it before going further.

You retired from the federal bench in 2020 after eighteen years, preceded by fourteen as an Assistant U.S. Attorney and nine in private practice. You are “semi-retired” in the sense that the calendar still has weight on it — mediations, mentees, the Federal Judicial Center panel — but the rhythm is your own now, and a meaningful part of it belongs to seven grandchildren in places you have to fly to.

Your stack is deliberately spare: Microsoft Word, Outlook, Zoom, Adobe Acrobat, Apple Contacts. A paper Filofax you have used since 1987. Yellow pads. Apple Calendar where it has to be shared with Walter. This is not a person who needs more software. It is a person who needs the right one or two pieces, picked with care.

The pain you named most directly is travel. The volume of confirmations, itineraries, hotel cards, and rental car reservations “is more than I can comfortably manage,” and you have missed appointments because the relevant email could not be found at the relevant moment. You called this the magic-wand problem. We took that seriously — most of this plan is built around it.

The second thing you'd hand off if you could is the photographs — fifty thousand digital ones scattered across devices, plus the boxes in the basement. We are going to tell you something that may sound counterintuitive: the answer here is less technology, not more, and most of it is paying someone else to do the part you'd never get to.

You're not behind. You have evaluated technology with the same eye you brought to evidence for forty-one years, and the things you flagged — hallucinated citations, the privacy of typed-in information, the displacement of judgment — are the right things to flag. We are going to tell you when not to use these tools as often as we tell you when to.

03 — How we picked these tools

Tools that survive a careful eye. No leaps of faith required.

You set the bar in your last note: you will not adopt anything you do not understand, and a “leap of faith” disqualifies a recommendation. We took that as the brief. Every tool below comes from a company with a long operating history, has a privacy posture we can point to in writing, and solves a problem you named in your own words.

We looked at roughly fifteen products in the travel and photo-management space before settling on these, and we ruled out a number of well-marketed names that would not have aged well in your hands.

Two of the recommendations are not software at all — one is a paid service that handles a project for you, and one is a careful re-use of something you already pay for. We think both are more useful than another subscription. The footnote at the end lists what we considered and cut, and why.

04 — What we think could help

A short list of tools worth your attention.

Four foundation pieces, two careful uses of AI. Foundation tools are the plumbing — travel, photos, passwords. The AI section is short on purpose; you do not need six AI tools and we would not respect your time by inventing reasons to recommend them.

Foundation 4 items
TripIt Pro
$49 / year (about $4 / mo)

What it is: A travel app with one trick that it has been refining for twenty years. When a confirmation email arrives — flight, hotel, rental car, restaurant reservation, anything — you forward it to plans@tripit.com. TripIt reads the email, understands what it is, and adds it to a single chronological itinerary for whatever trip it belongs to. Your phone then shows you, in order, where you are supposed to be next. Offline, too, which matters when you land somewhere without reliable signal.

Why it helps you: This is the answer to your magic-wand question. You said the volume of confirmations “is more than I can comfortably manage,” and that you've missed appointments because you couldn't find the relevant email at the relevant moment. The fix is not a better filing system in Outlook — it's getting those confirmations out of Outlook and into one timeline that knows what it's looking at. You and Walter can share trips so he sees the same itinerary. The Pro version, which we'd recommend, watches your flights and wakes you in the middle of the night when something is canceled — sometimes before the airline does. Nearly twenty million people use it, and it has been around since 2006, so the “startup that vanishes” risk is low.

You still have to forward the emails (or set up one Outlook rule that does it automatically). It can't book the trip — only organize what you've already booked.

Getting started — step by step
  1. On your iPhone, open the App Store (the blue icon with the “A” made of sticks). Tap the search tab at the bottom, type TripIt, and tap Get next to “TripIt: Travel Planner.” It will ask for your Face ID or password to install — that's normal.
  2. Open the app. Tap Sign Up. Use your regular email address (the Outlook one you use for travel bookings). Create a password. Write it down in the Filofax for now — we'll move it to 1Password later.
  3. Upgrade to Pro. After sign-up, the app will show you a “Try Pro Free” screen. Tap it. Choose the Annual plan ($49/year). It will ask for your Apple ID payment — that's the same card you use for the App Store. You get a 14-day free trial; your card won't be charged today.
  4. Add Walter. In the app, tap More (bottom right) → Travelers → Add Traveler. Enter Walter's name and email. He'll get an invitation to connect — when he accepts, you'll both see the same trips.
  5. Set up email forwarding in Outlook. This is the one step that makes TripIt work without you thinking about it. On your Mac, open Outlook. Go to the menu bar: Tools → Rules. Click the + button to add a new rule. Set it up as follows:
    • Rule name: TripIt auto-forward
    • When a new message arrives that meets all these conditions: From — Contains — type confirmation
    • Do the following: Forward To — type plans@tripit.com
    Click Save. From now on, most booking confirmations will automatically land in TripIt without you lifting a finger.
  6. Test it right now. Find a recent travel confirmation in your Outlook inbox — a flight, hotel, anything. Forward it manually to plans@tripit.com. Open the TripIt app on your iPhone. Within about 90 seconds, the trip should appear with the details organized on a single timeline. If it worked, the rule will handle the rest going forward.
If the rule catches emails that aren't travel-related, we can narrow it later — add a second condition like “Subject contains 'itinerary' or 'reservation' or 'booking.'” Start broad; tune later.
Apple Photos and iCloud+, used properly
$2.99 / mo for 200 GB · $9.99 / mo for 2 TB

What it is: Not a new app. The Photos app already on your iPhone and Mac, turned on properly, with enough iCloud storage to hold all fifty thousand pictures. Once it is set up — which is a one-time afternoon, not an ongoing project — every photo you and Walter take backs itself up, and you can search by typing words like “Hazel birthday” or “Cape Cod” into the search bar. Faces are recognized. Locations are remembered. It is the same software you've been ignoring; it just hasn't been switched on.

Why it helps you: You said anything involving “syncing” or “the cloud” is where you and Walter get stuck, and that your children's explanations make it worse. We are not going to recommend Google Photos or Mylio or any of the other products designed for people who enjoy this. You have an iPhone and a Mac. The Photos app on both already does what you need. The work is the one-time setup — turning on iCloud Photos, picking a storage tier large enough for fifty thousand pictures (2 TB), and confirming Walter's phone is signed in with shared albums. That is the entire project. Once done, it runs without you ever having to think about it again.

It will not organize the boxes of paper photos in the basement. That is the next item.

Getting started — step by step
  1. Upgrade your iCloud storage first. On your iPhone, open Settings (the gear icon). Tap your name at the very top → iCloud → Manage Account Storage → Change Storage Plan. Choose 2 TB ($9.99/month). This is enough for all fifty thousand photos, Walter's photos, your Mac backups, and years of future pictures. Confirm with Face ID.
  2. Turn on iCloud Photos on your iPhone. Go to Settings → Photos (scroll down to find it). Turn on iCloud Photos. Your phone will start uploading all your existing pictures in the background — this can take a day or two on your home Wi-Fi, and that's normal. You don't have to do anything while it works.
  3. Turn on iCloud Photos on your Mac. Open System Settings (click the Apple menu at the top-left of your screen → System Settings). Click Apple Account (your name at the top) → iCloud → Photos → turn on Sync this Mac. Again, the upload runs in the background.
  4. Set up a Shared Album with Walter. On your iPhone, open the Photos app. Tap the Albums tab at the bottom. Tap the + in the top-left → New Shared Album. Name it “Family photos — Margaret & Walter.” Tap Next, type Walter's email address, tap Create. He'll get an invitation on his iPhone — when he accepts, either of you can add photos the other person will see.
  5. Do Walter's phone too. Repeat steps 1–2 on Walter's iPhone. He should be signed into his own Apple Account (not yours). The Shared Album you just created will appear in his Photos app once he accepts the invitation.
  6. Try the search. Open Photos on your iPhone. Tap the Search tab (magnifying glass, bottom right). Type the name of a grandchild, or a place you've visited, or a word like “birthday.” Scroll through what it finds. This is the feature that makes fifty thousand photos manageable instead of overwhelming.
The first upload may take 24–48 hours. Leave your phone on Wi-Fi and plugged in overnight. You can check progress: Settings → Photos → look for the message at the bottom that says how many photos remain.
ScanMyPhotos (or a local equivalent) for the basement boxes
About $145 to fill-a-box (~1,800 photos), plus shipping

What it is: A scanning service. You order a prepaid box, pack the paper photographs into it (no sorting required), and mail it. They scan everything at archival resolution, return your originals, and deliver the digital files on a thumb drive or download. The whole process takes a few weeks. ScanMyPhotos has been doing this since 1990 and has scanned over a billion images; they are the most established in the category. If you'd prefer to hand the box to a person rather than a UPS driver, there are local archival services in the Pasadena area that do the same work for a higher per-photo price.

Why it helps you: You've been talking about this project for years and have made no progress. There is a reason: scanning thirty years of paper photographs yourself is a forty- to sixty-hour task with a flatbed scanner, and that is before any organizing. It will not happen. Paying a service to do it is the difference between this being a real archive your grandchildren will have, and a project that stays in the basement. Once the digital files come back, they go into the same Apple Photos library as everything else, and the search above works on them too. This is the single biggest “what I wish someone would just handle” item you raised, and the right answer is to actually have someone handle it.

The shipping bit will feel uncomfortable the first time. Pack one box first as a test — a few hundred photos you'd be sorry but not heartbroken to lose — and once it comes back well, send the rest.

Getting started — step by step
  1. Order one test box. On your Mac, open Safari and go to scanmyphotos.com. Click Order Now. Choose the “Fill-A-Box” option — this is the one where they send you an empty prepaid box and you fill it with up to approximately 1,800 photos. The cost is about $145. Enter your shipping address and payment. The box arrives in about a week.
  2. Pick your test batch. Go to the basement. Pull out 300–400 photographs you'd be sorry but not heartbroken to lose — duplicates, vacation shots from a trip you remember well, anything where you have the negatives elsewhere. This is your test run.
  3. Pack the box. Put the photos in face-up, loosely stacked. They don't need to be sorted, labeled, or organized. Include a note with your name and email. Seal the box and drop it at UPS (the shipping label is prepaid and included in the box).
  4. Wait 2–3 weeks. They'll email you when scanning is complete. You'll get a download link (or a thumb drive in the mail, depending on the option you chose).
  5. Import the scanned photos into Apple Photos. When the files arrive:
    • If they sent a thumb drive: plug it into your Mac. Open the Photos app. Go to File → Import. Select the thumb drive, select all the photos, and click Import All New Items.
    • If they sent a download link: click the link in the email, download the ZIP file. Double-click the ZIP file to unzip it (this creates a folder of photos on your Mac). Then open Photos → File → Import → select that folder.
    The scanned photos will now appear alongside all your digital ones and are searchable the same way.
  6. Check the quality. Look at 10–15 of the scanned images. Are they clear? Are they the right orientation? Are any missing? If the quality is good, order more boxes for the rest of the basement. If something's off, email their customer service before sending more — they're responsive.
Your originals come back. ScanMyPhotos returns every photograph in the same box. Nothing is kept or destroyed.
1Password
~$5 / mo individual · ~$8 / mo family (covers Walter)

What it is: A digital version of the Filofax page where you've been keeping logins, protected by one password you memorize. It fills in the rest automatically on your phone and Mac. It also refuses to fill a password into a website that looks legitimate but isn't — which is one of the more common scam patterns aimed at people in your demographic.

Why it helps you: Two reasons. First, the paper list is the friction point behind a lot of the small daily aggravations — logging into the airline site to check a flight, signing into a portal a doctor sent you, recovering the password that you wrote down two Filofaxes ago. Second, and more important: a former federal judge is a high-value target for phishing. The behavior of 1Password — it simply will not autofill on a fraudulent site — is a quiet line of defense you don't have to remember to use. Here's how that works in practice: a scam email sends you to a page that looks exactly like your bank's login. You would not notice the difference. But 1Password checks the actual web address, not the appearance — and if the address is wrong, it refuses to fill in your password. You'd see an empty login form where it normally fills itself in, and that silence is the warning.

It will not, and should not, replace the Filofax for the things the Filofax does well. The Filofax is your calendar and your notebook. 1Password just absorbs the password page.

Getting started — step by step
  1. Sign up on your Mac. Open Safari and go to 1password.com. Click Try Free (top right). Choose the Families plan ($4.99/month, 14-day free trial). Enter your email address and create an account. It will ask you to create a “master password” — this is the one password you'll memorize. Pick something long and personal that you won't forget. Write it in the Filofax.
  2. Download the Emergency Kit. After sign-up, 1Password will show you an “Emergency Kit” — a PDF with your Secret Key (a long code unique to your account). Print it. Store the printed copy somewhere safe — not the Filofax, somewhere more secure (a safe, a locked drawer). You'll need this if you ever set up 1Password on a new device.
  3. Install the Safari extension. 1Password will prompt you to install its Safari extension. Click Install and follow the prompts. Once installed, you'll see a small key icon in Safari's toolbar. This is what fills in passwords for you automatically.
  4. Install on your iPhone. Open the App Store on your iPhone. Search 1Password. Tap Get. Open the app and sign in with your email, master password, and the Secret Key from the Emergency Kit. Then go to Settings (on your iPhone) → Passwords → Password Options → turn on 1Password as your AutoFill provider.
  5. Invite Walter. On your Mac, go to 1password.com and sign in. Click your name (top right) → Manage Family → Invite. Enter Walter's email. He'll get an invitation to create his own account under your family plan.
  6. Migrate your most-used passwords first. Don't try to move everything from the Filofax at once. Start with the ten accounts you use most often — your email, the airline, the bank, the pharmacy portal, Amazon, and so on. Each time you log into one of these sites over the next week, 1Password will ask if you want to save the password. Say yes. Within a week or two, the Filofax password page becomes a backup instead of your daily reference.
You don't have to move every password at once. Let 1Password learn them as you use them naturally. Within a month, most of your daily logins will be in it. The Filofax stays as your backup — you're not throwing it away.
AI 2 items
NotebookLM
Free · made by Google

What it is: A research tool from Google with one important property: it only answers from documents you upload to it. It does not bring in information from the open internet, and it does not invent citations — when it tells you something, it shows you the exact sentence in the source document where it came from. You upload PDFs or paste in text, and then ask questions about that material in plain English.

Why it helps you: You said the cumulative correspondence around your children's lives — “various medical, educational, and logistical things they share with us” — is sitting in disorganized email threads, and you would have benefited from a real system thirty years ago. NotebookLM is a way to get one now without building it. Periodically, copy the relevant emails into a notebook for each adult child's family. Then when something comes up — “what did Dr. So-and-so recommend for the appointment in March,” or “what was the school's name” — you ask, and you get the answer with the email it came from highlighted. The reason we are recommending this one and not ChatGPT for this purpose is precisely the concern you raised about hallucinated citations. NotebookLM is structurally constrained to its sources; it cannot fabricate authority because it has none of its own to draw on. Your data is not used to train Google's models.

It is a reading and reference tool, not a writing tool. And it requires the one-time work of putting the relevant documents into it. We'd suggest starting with one family — the one whose situation is currently most active — and not touching the others until that one feels useful.

Getting started — step by step
  1. Open NotebookLM. On your Mac, open Safari and go to notebooklm.google.com. Sign in with a Google account. If you don't have one, you can create one for free — you won't need to use Gmail or anything else from Google, just the account to sign in.
  2. Create your first notebook. Click New Notebook. Name it something simple like “Sarah's family — medical & school.” (Use whichever child's situation is most active right now.)
  3. Add your sources. Click Add Source (the “+” button in the Sources panel on the left). You have three good options:
    • Paste text: Open Outlook, find 5–6 relevant emails from that child (medical updates, school information, logistics discussions). Select the text of each email, copy it, and paste it into NotebookLM as a new source. Name each source something you'll recognize (“Dr. Rivera appointment — March 2026”).
    • Upload a PDF: If you have documents saved as PDFs (a school report, a medical summary), drag the file from your Mac into the Sources panel.
    • Google Docs: If any of the information lives in a Google Doc (unlikely for you, but in case), you can connect it directly.
  4. Start with five or six sources. Don't try to upload everything. Five or six emails from the past year for one child is enough to see whether this is useful. You can always add more later.
  5. Ask it something real. In the chat panel on the right side, type a question you actually want the answer to. See the examples below.
Try these — copy and paste into NotebookLM
What did Dr. Rivera recommend at the appointment in March? Include the exact email where you found this.

NotebookLM will highlight the exact sentence in the source email. If the answer isn't in the emails you uploaded, it will say so — it won't guess.

Another example
What school did they mention for next year, and what were the deadlines?

Good for when a child mentioned something in an email three months ago and you can't find the thread.

One more
Give me a timeline of all the medical appointments and recommendations mentioned in these emails, in order.

This is especially useful before a phone call with your child — you get a one-page summary of everything they've shared, with the sources cited.

ChatGPT — used carefully, for documents only
Conditional Free tier handles most of this · $20 / mo Plus if you find you want it

What it is: The application your son showed you at Christmas. We are recommending it for one specific use — a “translator” for confusing documents. You photograph a page (a Medicare statement, a bank notice, a long-form medical letter) and ask what it actually says, what to look for, and what to ask the relevant professional about it. It is good at this. It is not good, as you observed, at writing in your voice — and we agree with you about that.

Why it helps you: Two specific guardrails are why we're recommending this carefully rather than enthusiastically. First, never type or photograph anything into it that involves a mediation, a mentee's confidential matter, or anything covered by a duty of confidence. A federal court ruled this year that conversations with consumer AI tools are not protected by privilege and may be discoverable. That is exactly the concern you raised, and it is correct. Second, opt out of training in the privacy settings the first time you sign in — by default these systems retain inputs, and the opt-out matters. With those two rules, the residual use case is genuinely useful: a tool that helps you read a confusing personal document on your own time, before involving anyone else. Your instinct that it lacks voice for letter-writing is also correct, which is why we are not recommending it for the letters you write to your grandchildren and your correspondents. Those are the part of your life that should remain entirely yours.

It will not improve your writing, and it should not be allowed near your professional work. Treat it the way you'd treat a clerk you didn't hire and didn't vet — useful for a quick gloss on a document, not for anything that bears your name.

Getting started — step by step
  1. Install on your iPhone. Open the App Store. Search ChatGPT. The correct one is by “OpenAI” — it has a black-and-white circular logo. Tap Get.
  2. Create an account. Open the app. Tap Sign Up. Use your regular email. Create a password. (The free tier is enough to start — you do not need to pay $20/month unless you find yourself using it often.)
  3. Immediately turn off training. This is important. In the app, tap your profile icon (top right) → Settings → Data Controls → turn off the toggle that says “Improve the model for everyone.” This means your conversations are not used to train OpenAI's models. Do this before you use it for anything.
  4. Try it with a real document. Find a confusing piece of mail — a Medicare Explanation of Benefits, a bank fee notice, a letter from a financial advisor with jargon you'd rather not call and ask about. Take a clear photo of it with your iPhone camera (just use the regular Camera app). Then open ChatGPT, tap the + button next to the message box, tap Photo Library, select the photo you just took, and type your question. See the examples below.
Try this — copy and paste after attaching a photo of the document
I'm attaching a photo of a Medicare statement I received. Please explain in plain English: what does this actually say? What was covered, what was denied, and what (if anything) do I owe? If anything seems unusual, tell me what question I should ask when I call.

You can replace “Medicare statement” with whatever the document is — a bank notice, a tax form, a medical explanation. The pattern is the same: photograph, attach, ask what it means and what to do next.

For a financial document
This is a notice from my brokerage about a change to my account. I don't understand the language. Please explain what they're actually telling me, whether I need to do anything, and what I should ask my financial advisor about this.
When something feels wrong
I received this letter and I'm not sure if it's legitimate or a scam. Can you look at the language and tell me what signals suggest it's real vs. fraudulent? What should I look for?

ChatGPT is quite good at spotting the telltale signs of scam letters — urgency language, fake reference numbers, unusual return addresses. It's not a substitute for calling the institution directly, but it's a useful first read.

04B — What we considered and ruled out

A short list of tools we looked at and didn't pick.

A few well-marketed options we considered and decided against. Each of these is a tool a knowledgeable friend might reasonably suggest; we don't think any of them is the right fit for you specifically.

Google Photos

The single best photo search on the market, but it would mean adopting a Google account ecosystem alongside the Apple one you already use, and you told us anything involving “syncing” or “the cloud” is where things break down. Apple Photos is good enough and is one fewer system to learn.

Mylio Photos

Excellent for power users with photographs spread across drives and devices, and privacy-first by design. But the App Store reviews are clear that it has a learning curve, and you'd be the one maintaining it. The setup cost outweighs the benefit for your case.

Wanderlog

A more visually polished travel planner than TripIt with better maps, but it requires more active planning and a separate forwarding email per trip. TripIt's twenty years of email-parsing maturity and one universal forwarding address are worth more to you than Wanderlog's nicer interface.

Legacybox for the photo scanning

Heavily advertised on Sirius and elsewhere. We looked carefully — turnaround times reportedly stretch ten to twelve weeks, the pricing model is rigid, and customer-service complaints are well documented. ScanMyPhotos is the more boring, more reliable choice.

Claude (Anthropic) instead of ChatGPT

A reasonable alternative we considered. Honestly, for your specific use case — occasional document explanation — the practical difference is small, and ChatGPT's iPhone app is meaningfully better at reading photographed documents. Both have the same privacy caveats; opt out either way. If you ended up preferring Claude's voice, it would be a fine substitute.

07 — Guardrails

What never goes into these tools. And how to get out if you want to.

You asked the right questions about privacy and trust. Here are the answers, plainly — what's safe, what's not, and how to walk away from any of this if it doesn't feel right.

Things never to type or photograph into any AI tool

This applies to ChatGPT, NotebookLM, and anything else that takes text input. NotebookLM is safer than most (it doesn't send your data to the open internet), but the discipline is the same everywhere.

Never
Anything involving a mediation, a mentee's matter, or a duty of confidence. Consumer AI tools are not protected by privilege. A federal court confirmed this in 2025. If it could be discoverable, it doesn't go in.
Never
Social Security numbers, bank account numbers, credit card numbers, or medical record numbers. Not yours. Not Walter's. Not a grandchild's. Not even to “ask what a form means” — crop or cover the number before photographing the document.
Never
Passwords or login credentials. ChatGPT does not need your bank password to explain a bank statement. If a tool ever asks for a password, close the window — that is not normal behavior.
Be careful with
Full names of grandchildren combined with their schools, addresses, or medical details. Use first names only if you need to reference a child. “My granddaughter, age 8, at a public school in Boston” is enough context without being identifying.
Safe to use
Personal documents that are already yours — Medicare statements, bank fee notices, tax summaries, travel confirmations, medical explanations from your own doctors. These are the intended use case. Just crop out account numbers before photographing.

How to know when AI is wrong

It will be wrong sometimes. Here's how to spot it:

Watch for
Confident answers to questions that should have hedging. If you ask about a medical recommendation and it gives a definitive answer without saying “consult your doctor,” be skeptical. The more confident it sounds, the more you should verify.
Watch for
Citations you can't find. In NotebookLM, every answer should have a highlighted source on the left. If it gives an answer without highlighting a source, it may be extrapolating rather than quoting. Ask it: “Show me exactly where in my documents you found that.”
Watch for
Agreeing with you too easily. If you say “I think this Medicare statement means I owe $400” and it says “Yes, that's correct,” double-check. These tools are biased toward agreeing with you. Ask instead: “What does this Medicare statement say I owe, and how did you arrive at that number?”

ChatGPT
